“Amer Fort”

Amer Fort, situated in Amer, Rajasthan, India, is a historic fortification located 11 kilometers from Jaipur, the capital of Rajasthan. Covering an area of 4 square kilometers, Amer is renowned for its artistic style and stands as the primary tourist attraction in Jaipur. Positioned atop a hill, the fort boasts large ramparts, gates, and cobbled paths, providing scenic views of Maota Lake. This lake serves as the primary water source for the Amer Palace, enhancing the fort's significance and contributing to its picturesque surroundings.

Amer Palace showcases exemplary Rajput and Mughal architectural influences. Crafted from red sandstone and marble, the opulent palace spans four levels, featuring the Diwan-e-Aam, Diwan-e-Khas, Sheesh Mahal (mirror palace or Jai Mandir), and Sukh Niwas. Notable for its artificial climate created by winds over a water cascade, the palace is often referred to as Amer Fort. Serving as the residence for Rajput Maharajas and their families, it includes a temple dedicated to Shila Devi near Ganesh Gate. The palace's design accommodates 12 rooms for Raja Man Singh's 12 queens, with separate staircases connecting to the king's room.

Situated atop the Cheel ka Teela (Hill of Eagles) in the Aravalli range, Amer Palace and Jaigarh Fort form a connected complex. Linked by a subterranean passage, designed as an escape route during war, it facilitated the royal family's shift from Amer Fort to the more formidable Jaigarh Fort. Amer Palace attracts an annual tourist footfall of 1.4 million, with 5,000 visitors daily, according to the Department of Archaeology and Museums. Recognized for its historical significance, Amer Fort, along with other forts, was declared a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2013.

History Of Fort:

Amber was a Meena state, ruled by a Susawat clan. After Kakil Deo defeated the Susawats he made Amber the capital of Dhundhar after Khoh. Kakil Deo was a son of Dulherai.

In early times, the state of Jaipur was known as Amber or Dhundhar and was controlled by Meena chiefs of five different tribes. They were under suzerainty of the Bargurjar Rajput Raja of Deoti. Later a Kachhwaha prince, Dulha Rai, destroyed the sovereignty of Meenas and also defeated Bargurjars of Deoli and took Dhundhar fully under Kachwaha rule.

The Amber Fort was originally built by Raja Man Singh. Jai Singh I expanded it in the early 1600's. Improvements and additions were made by successive rulers over the next 150 years, until the Kachwahas shifted their capital to Jaipur during the time of Sawai Jai Singh II, in 1727.

During the medieval era, Amer was referred to as Dhundar, signifying a sacrificial mount in the western frontiers. Ruled by the Kachwahas from the 11th century to 1727 AD, the history of Amer is intimately connected to this dynasty. For nearly seven centuries, from 1037 AD until the capital's relocation to Jaipur, the Kachwahas established and ruled their empire from Amer. The city played a pivotal role in shaping the historical narrative of the Kachwaha rulers until the transition of the capital to Jaipur.

Architecture:

The Palace is divided into six separate but main sections each with its own entry gate and courtyard. The main entry is through the Suraj Pol (Sun Gate) which leads to the first main courtyard. This was the place where armies would hold victory parades with their war bounty on their return from battles, which were also witnessed by the Royal family's womenfolk through the latticed windows.[24] This gate was built exclusively[clarification needed] and was provided with guards as it was the main entry into the palace. It faced east towards the rising sun, hence the name. Royal cavalcades and dignitaries entered the palace through this gate.

Jaleb Chowk is an Arabic phrase meaning a place for soldiers to assemble. This is one of the four courtyards of Amer Palace, which was built during Sawai Jai Singh's reign (1693–1743 AD). Maharaja's personal bodyguards held parades here under the command of the army commander or Fauj Bakshi. The Maharaja used to inspect the guards contingent. Adjacent to the courtyard were the horse stables, with the upper-level rooms occupied by the guards.

Conservation:

Six forts of Rajasthan, namely, Amber Fort, Chittor Fort, Gagron Fort, Jaisalmer Fort, Kumbhalgarh and Ranthambore Fort were included in the UNESCO World Heritage Site list during the 37th meeting of the World Heritage Committee in Phnom Penh during June 2013. They were recognized as a serial cultural property and examples of Rajput military hill architecture.

Amer town, an essential gateway to Amer Palace, has transformed into a heritage town, relying on tourism with 4,000 to 5,000 daily visitors during peak season. Encompassing 4 square kilometers, the town features eighteen temples, three Jain mandirs, and three mosques. Recognized as one of the 100 endangered sites worldwide by the World Monument Fund (WMF), it benefits from the Robert Wilson Challenge Grant for conservation. In 2005, the fort housed around 87 elephants, but some were reported to be suffering from malnutrition. The town's cultural richness and conservation efforts contribute to its significance.

Conservation works have been undertaken at the Amer Palace grounds at a cost of Rs 40 crores (US$8.88 million) by the Amer Development and Management Authority (ADMA). However, these renovation works have been a subject of intense debate and criticism with respect to their suitability to maintain and retain the historicity and architectural features of the ancient structures. Another issue which has been raised is the commercialization of the place.

During a film shoot at Amer Fort, a 500-year-old canopy was damaged, the old limestone roof of Chand Mahal was demolished, holes were drilled to install sets, and large quantities of sand were spread in Jaleb Chowk, violating the Rajasthan Monuments, Archaeological Sites, and Antique Act (1961). The Rajasthan High Court's Jaipur Bench intervened, halting the film shoot. The court observed that authorities, swayed by financial allurements, had turned a blind eye to the historical significance of protected monuments, highlighting the unfortunate trend of exploiting such sites for income.
