“Chhitorgarh Fort”

The Chittorgarh (literally Chittor Fort), also known as Chittod Fort, is one of the largest forts in India. It is a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The fort was the capital of Mewar and is located in the present-day city of Chittorgarh. It sprawls over a hill 180 m (590.6 ft) in height spread over an area of 280 ha (691.9 acres) above the plains of the valley drained by the Berach River. The fort covers 65 historic structures, which include four palaces, 19 large temples, 20 large water bodies, 4 memorials and a few victory towers.

History Of Fort:

Chittorgarh, originally called Chitrakut, is said to have been built by the western branch of the Mauryan ruler Chitrangada Mori. The fort's name is believed to be derived from its builder. Small Buddhist stupas dating back to the 9th century have been found near Jaimal Patta lake, adding to the historical richness of the region.

The Guhila ruler Bappa Rawal is credited with capturing the fort of Chittor in either 728 CE or 734 CE. One account suggests that he obtained the fort as part of a dowry. Alternatively, according to other versions of the legend, Bappa Rawal seized the fort from the Moris. Historian R. C. Majumdar posits that the Moris were in control of Chittor when the Arabs invaded north-western India around 725 CE. The Arabs defeated the Moris but were, in turn, defeated by a confederacy that included Bappa Rawal. R. V. Somani theorizes that Bappa Rawal may have been part of the army of Nagabhata I. However, some historians question the historicity of this legend, arguing that the Guhilas did not have control over Chittor until the reign of the later ruler Allata. The earliest Guhila inscription found at Chittor dates back to the mid-13th century during the reign of Tejasimha and mentions "Chitrakuta-maha-durga" (the great fort of Chittor).

The siege of Chittorgarh in 1303, led by Alauddin Khalji of the Delhi Sultanate, resulted in the capture of the fort after an eight-month-long campaign. Historical accounts by Amir Khusrow mention a massacre of 30,000 local Hindus ordered by Alauddin after the conquest. While some legends suggest the invasion was to capture the queen Padmini, modern historians question the authenticity of these tales.

Following the conquest, Alauddin assigned Chittorgarh to his young son Khizr Khan, renaming the fort "Khizrabad." The administration was overseen by Malik Shahin due to Khizr Khan's young age. Subsequent rulers, including Rana Hammir, regained control, and Chittorgarh flourished again.

Rana Sanga's reign in the 15th century marked a resurgence of Rajput power, expanding Mewar's influence. However, after a series of victories, Rana Sanga faced defeat at the Battle of Khanwa against Babur, marking the establishment of Mughal rule in India.

In 1535, Chittorgarh faced another siege by Bahadur Shah, Sultan of Gujarat, resulting in the tragic jauhar (mass self-immolation) by Rajput women. The final siege in 1567 by Mughal Emperor Akbar led to the fall of Chittorgarh after a four-month-long struggle, culminating in jauhar and the fort's conquest. A peace treaty in 1616 between Jahangir and Amar Singh resulted in Chittorgarh being returned to the latter. The fort underwent refurbishment in 1905 during the British Raj.

Architecture:

Chittorgarh Fort, located in the state of Rajasthan, India, is renowned for its impressive architecture and historical significance. The fort stands on a hilltop, covering a vast area and is surrounded by a series of walls and gates. Here are key features of the architecture of Chittorgarh Fort:

Multiple Gates: Chittorgarh Fort is accessed through a series of seven gates, each with its own significance and name. The main gates include the Padan Pol, Bhairon Pol, and Hanuman Pol, each serving different purposes and showcasing unique architectural elements.

Vijay Stambh (Victory Tower): One of the most iconic structures within the fort is the Vijay Stambh, a nine-story tower built by Maharana Kumbha in the 15th century. The tower is adorned with intricate carvings and sculptures, depicting scenes from Hindu mythology and the Rajput history.

Kirti Stambh (Tower of Fame): Another notable tower within the fort is the Kirti Stambh, dedicated to Jain Tirthankaras. It is a seven-story tower adorned with Jain sculptures and carvings, showcasing the influence of Jainism in the region.

Palaces and Temples: The fort houses various palaces and temples that exhibit Rajput and Mewar architecture. The Rana Kumbha Palace, Padmini Palace, and Fateh Prakash Palace are examples of structures within the fort that showcase the opulence and architectural prowess of the Rajputs.

Jauhar Kund and Rani Padmini's Palace: The Jauhar Kund, where mass self-immolation (jauhar) took place during times of siege, is a significant historical site. Rani Padmini's Palace, associated with the legendary queen, is known for its architecture and strategic location offering panoramic views of the surrounding landscape.

Water Reservoirs: Chittorgarh Fort features several water reservoirs, including Gaumukh Kund and Rana Kumbha's Palace Tank, which were crucial for the water supply during times of siege.

Bassie and Towers: The fort has various bastions and towers strategically located along its walls, providing vantage points for defense and surveillance.

Architecture from Different Periods: The fort reflects architectural styles from different periods of its history, ranging from the 7th century to the 19th century. The structures showcase a blend of Rajput, Mughal, and Jain architectural influences.

Precincts :

Precints is the area within the walls or perceived boundaries of a particular building or place.

Chittorgarh Fort, resembling the shape of a fish, spans 13 km in circumference and covers 700 acres. Its difficult approach involves a zig-zag ascent of over 1 km from the plains, crossing a limestone bridge over the Gambhiri River with ten arches. Dominated by two tall towers, the fort houses numerous palaces and temples within its precincts, some now in ruins. The fortification is surrounded by a 13 km wall, making it nearly inaccessible due to a 45° hill slope. The ascent passes through seven gates, built by Rana Kumbha, named Paidal Pol, Bhairon Pol, Hanuman Pol, Ganesh Pol, Jorla Pol, Laxman Pol, and Ram Pol. The complex comprises 65 historic structures, including palaces, temples, memorials, and water bodies, reflecting two major construction phases in the 5th and 15th centuries. The fort's unique topography and architectural elements contribute to its significance.

In addition to the palace complex situated on the highest and most secure terrain in the west of Chittorgarh Fort, several other significant structures were constructed during the second phase of its development. Notable among them are the Kumbha Shyam Temple, Mira Bai Temple, Adi Varah Temple, Shringar Chauri Temple, and the Vijay Stambh memorial. This construction phase, primarily attributed to the 15th century under the Sisodia Rajputs, showcases a relatively pure Rajput architectural style with minimal eclecticism, incorporating features like vaulted substructures borrowed from Sultanate architecture.

The fort's 4.5 km walls, integrated with circular enforcements, are crafted from dressed stone masonry in lime mortar, towering 500 m above the plain. Access to the fort is restricted by seven massive stone gates, flanked by hexagonal or octagonal towers, leading through narrow defense passages. The final gate grants entry into the palace area, featuring residential and official structures. The Rana Kumbha Mahal, the palace of Rana Kumbha, is a large Rajput domestic structure, incorporating the Kanwar Pade Ka Mahal and the later palace of the poet Mira Bai. Over subsequent centuries, the palace area expanded with additions like the Ratan Singh Palace (1528–1531) and Fateh Prakash, also known as Badal Mahal (1885–1930). These structures contribute to the rich architectural tapestry of Chittorgarh Fort.

While Chittorgarh Fort predominantly features Hindu temples like the Kalikamata Temple (8th century), Kshemankari Temple (825–850), Kumbha Shyam Temple (1448), and Adbuthnath Temple (15th–16th century), it also hosts Jain structures such as Sattaees Devari, Shringar Chauri (1448), and Sat Bis Devri (mid-15th century). Notable Jain monuments include the Kirti Stambh (12th century) and Vijay Stambha (1433–1468), towering at 24 m and 37 m, ensuring visibility across the fort. Additionally, the fort houses a contemporary municipal ward with around 3,000 inhabitants near Ratan Singh Tank at the northern end of the property.
