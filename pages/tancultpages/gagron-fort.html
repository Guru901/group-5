“Gagron Fort”

Gagron Fort, located in the Jhalawar district of Rajasthan, India, is a notable hill and water fort situated in the Hadoti region. Constructed in the twelfth century by Rajput king Bijaldev Singh Dod, the fort exemplifies a combination of hill and water fortification. Throughout its history, Gagron Fort was under the control of rulers such as Sher Shah and Akbar. Positioned at the confluence of the Ahu River and Kali Sindh River, the fort is surrounded by water on three sides and has a moat on the fourth side, earning it the name "Jaladurg" or Water Fort in Hindi/Rajasthani. Recognized for its historical and architectural significance, Gagron Fort was designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2013 as part of the Hill Forts in Rajasthan.

History Of Fort:

Gagron Fort, believed to have been constructed during the twelfth century by King Bijaldev, was under the rule of the Khinchi kingdom for approximately 300 years. Despite the precise construction date being shrouded in mystery, historians estimate that the fort's construction spanned from the seventh century to the fourteenth century. The fort's historical significance and architectural features contribute to its mystique, attracting attention and interest from historians and visitors alike.

The final ruler of Gagron Fort was King Achal Das Khinchi. In a historical account from the medieval period, the fort faced an invasion by Muslim rulers from Malwa. In 1423, Sultan Hoshang Shah led an attack on the fort with a formidable army comprising 30 thousand horsemen and 84 elephant riders. Realizing the overwhelming odds against him, Achal Das Khinchi chose not to surrender but fought valiantly until he lost his life, adhering to Rajput tradition. In the face of impending capture by the Sultan's forces, many women within the fort performed jauhar, a ritualistic self-immolation, to preserve their honor. Gagron Fort reportedly witnessed 14 battles and two instances of queens performing jauhar.

The fort has also been conquered by Sher Shah and Akbar. Akbar also reportedly made this fort a headquarter and later gave it to Prathviraj of Bikaner as a part of his estate.

Architecture:

Gagron Fort, situated in Jhalawar, Rajasthan, is renowned for its unique hill and water fort architecture.

Location: Built on the confluence of the Ahu River and Kali Sindh River, Gagron Fort is surrounded by water on three sides, and a moat on the fourth, earning it the name Jaladurg (Water Fort).

Design: The fort's design takes advantage of the natural landscape, with strategic placement at the confluence enhancing its defensive capabilities.

Hill and Water Fort: Gagron Fort is a rare example of a hill and water fort, utilizing both natural terrain and artificial defenses to fortify the stronghold.

Construction Material: The fort is constructed using local stone and exhibits a blend of architectural styles reflecting the cultural influences of different periods.

Defensive Structures: The fort features defensive structures such as bastions, walls, and gateways strategically positioned to safeguard against external threats.

Confluence Fortification: The fort's unique location at the meeting point of two rivers adds an additional layer of defense, making it challenging for invaders to approach from certain directions.

Moat: The fort is surrounded by a moat, contributing to its defensive architecture and making it a formidable stronghold.

Cultural Significance: Gagron Fort's architectural elements bear witness to the historical events and battles that transpired within its walls, adding to its cultural and historical significance.

Structures:

Gagron Fort is surrounded by water on three sides and a moat filled with water on the fourth side. It is constructed on the confluence of Ahu River and Kali Sindh River. The fort also boasts three ramparts as opposed to traditional forts that have only two. The towers of the fort are blended with Mukundara Hills of the Vindhya Range. The mountain that the fort sits on is itself the foundation of the fort. The fort also has two main entrances. One gate leads towards the river, while the other gate leads towards the hilly road.
