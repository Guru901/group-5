“Junagarh Fort”

Junagarh Fort, located in Bikaner, Rajasthan, India, has a rich history dating back to the fifth century BC when it was ruled by Raja Dhaj, Ror Kumar. Originally named Chintamani, the fort was later renamed Junagarh, meaning "Old Fort" in the early 20th century when the ruling family shifted to Lalgarh Palace outside the fort limits. Unlike many forts in Rajasthan, Junagarh is not built on a hilltop. The fort has played a central role in shaping the modern city of Bikaner, and its historical significance contributes to the city's development around it.

The fort complex was built under the supervision of Karan Chand, the Prime Minister of Raja Rai Singh, the sixth ruler of Bikaner, who ruled from 1571 to 1611 AD. Construction of the walls and associated moat commenced in 1589 and was completed in 1594. It was built outside the original fort of the city (the first fort built by Rao Bikaji), about 1.5 kilometres (0.93 mi) from the city centre. Some remnants of the old fort are preserved near the Lakshmi Narayan temple.

Historical records reveal that despite the repeated attacks by enemies to capture the fort, it was not taken, except for a lone one-day occupation by Kamran Mirza. Kamran was the second son of the Mughal Emperor Babur who attacked Bikaner in 1534, which was then ruled by Rao Jait Singh.

Spanning 5.28 hectares, the Junagarh Fort precinct is adorned with palaces, temples, and pavilions. Within this expansive space, a blend of architectural styles reflects a composite culture, showcasing the diverse influences and historical layers that contribute to the fort's rich heritage.

History Of Fort:

Before the construction of the present Junagarh Fort, a stone fort was erected in 1478 by Rao Bika, the second son of Maharaja Rao Jodha, founder of Jodhpur. Bika, unable to inherit his father's territory, established his kingdom at Bikaner in the region known as "Jungladesh." The city, deemed an oasis on the trade route between Central Asia and the Gujarat coast, prospered due to adequate spring water sources. Bikaner's fortunes flourished under Raja Rai Singhji, the sixth ruler, who accepted Mughal suzerainty and played a prominent role in their court. Rai Singhji's successful war exploits and jagirs granted him significant revenue, enabling the construction of Junagarh Fort. The fort's formal foundation ceremony occurred on February 17, 1589, and its completion was marked on January 17, 1594. Rai Singhji's expertise in arts and architecture is evident in the fort's design, making it an outstanding example of composite architecture and a unique artistic center in the Thar desert.

Architecture:

This magnificent fort sprawls across a vast expanse, measuring 1,500 ft in length and 750 ft in width, rising 250 ft above its surroundings. Its formidable triple-ring defense structure begins with a 15 ft tall outer wall at the base. The fort's upper bastions and towers create an inner defensive perimeter extending about 2.5 miles. Boasting an impressive 99 bastions, with 92 built or extensively reconstructed between 1633 and 1647, it stands as a testament to architectural prowess. The fort's four fortified entrances from the townside, one of which was once guarded by a cannon, showcase its strategic design.

Karan Singh, who reigned from 1631 to 1639 under Mughal suzerainty, constructed the Karan Mahal palace. Subsequent rulers, including Anup Singh (1669–98), expanded the fort complex, adding new palaces and the Zenana quarter. Anup Singh renovated Karan Mahal, transforming it into the Anup Mahal with a Diwan-i-Am (public audience hall). Gaj Singh (1746–1787) refurbished the Chandra Mahal (Moon palace), and Surat Singh (1787–1828) adorned the audience hall with glass and vibrant paintwork. Dungar Singh (1872–1887) built the Badal Mahal (Weather palace), depicting falling rain and clouds. Ganga Singh (1887–1943) contributed the Ganga Niwas Palace, designed by Sir Samuel Swinton Jacob. Ganga Singh's son, Sadul Singh, succeeded him in 1943 but acceded to the Union of India in 1949, passing away in 1950.

Bikaner fell under the suzerainty of the British Raj through a treaty signed in 1818, prompting the Maharajas of Bikaner to invest significantly in the renovation of Junagarh fort. However, in the 18th century, before the treaty, internal conflicts erupted between Bikaner and Jodhpur rulers, as well as among other Thakur. British troops intervened to quell these disputes. During an attack by the Jodhpur army, the fort's eastern entrance and southern rampart suffered damage, evidenced by cannonball marks on the southern facade.

Ganga Singh, a prominent king among the Rajasthan princes, stood out as a favorite of the British Raj, earning the title of Knight Commander of the Star of India. His significant contributions included serving on the Imperial War Cabinet, representing the country at the Imperial First World War Conferences and the Versailles Peace Conference. Despite his awareness of the shifting fortunes in World War II, he passed away in 1943 before its conclusion. Ganga Singh played a crucial role in Junagarh's building activity, adding separate halls for public and private audiences in the Ganga Mahal and a durbar hall for formal functions. The Lalgarh Palace, designed by Swinton, became his new residence in 1902, and the royal family still resides there, having converted it into a heritage hotel.

How It Is Today:

Today, the Jaisalmer Fort confronts numerous threats due to population growth. Challenges include water seepage, a lack of civic amenities, dilapidated structures, and seismic activity around Trikuta Hill. Unique in its construction atop weak sedimentary rock, the fort's foundation is vulnerable to seepage, resulting in the deterioration of significant sections, including the Queen's Palace (Rani Ka Mahal) and parts of the outer boundary wall and lower pitching walls.
